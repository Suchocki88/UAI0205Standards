Understanding AI: A Conceptual Approach
Chapter 2, Lesson 2.5: From Chaos to Comedy - Lab Day (Open-Ended Investigation)

Lesson 2.5 is a hands-on Lab Day where students conduct open-ended investigations into AI capabilities through systematic experimentation. Students test structured vs. unstructured prompts (Part A) and explore knowledge boundaries across different domains (Part B). This inquiry-based approach aligns with standards emphasizing scientific practices, research skills, collaborative investigation, data collection, pattern analysis, and evidence-based reasoning.

Crosscutting Concepts

Patterns
Observed patterns of forms and events guide organization and classification, and they prompt questions about relationships and the factors that influence them. Different patterns may be observed at each of the scales at which a system is studied and can provide evidence for causality in explanations of phenomena.
Lesson 2.5 Alignment: Students observe multiple pattern types: how prompt structure affects responses (structured prompts ? clearer outputs), how training data affects knowledge boundaries (common topics ? strong knowledge, niche topics ? weak knowledge), and meta-patterns in AI capabilities (system performs better when tasks match training conditions). Patterns provide evidence for causal explanations: training data determines what AI knows.
Cause and Effect: Mechanism and Explanation
Events have causes, sometimes simple, sometimes multifaceted. A major activity of science is investigating and explaining causal relationships and the mechanisms by which they are mediated. Cause and effect relationships may be used to predict phenomena in natural or designed systems.
Lesson 2.5 Alignment: Students identify cause-effect relationships through experimentation: structured prompts (cause) ? clearer responses (effect); common training data (cause) ? stronger knowledge (effect); rare topics (cause) ? weaker performance (effect). Understanding these mechanisms helps predict: "AI will likely struggle with this obscure topic because training data probably underrepresents it."
Systems and System Models
Systems may interact with other systems; they may have sub-systems and be a part of larger complex systems. Models can be used to predict the behavior of a system.
Lesson 2.5 Alignment: Students develop mental models of AI systems through observation: AI is a system that takes inputs (prompts), processes through learned patterns (subsystem: training data + algorithms), and produces outputs (responses). This model predicts behavior: clearer inputs and well-trained domains yield better outputs; vague inputs and rare domains yield weaker outputs.
Structure and Function
The way in which an object or living organism is shaped and its substructure determine many of its properties and functions.
Lesson 2.5 Alignment: Students discover how AI system structure (trained on text data, uses pattern matching) determines its functions and limitations: good at text tasks with training data coverage, struggles with tasks requiring knowledge beyond training, responsive to prompt structure that matches training patterns.
Stability and Change
Much of science deals with constructing explanations of how things change and how they remain stable. Explanations of stability and change in natural or designed systems can be constructed by examining the changes over time and processes at different scales.
Lesson 2.5 Alignment: Students observe stability in AI behavior: consistent patterns across trials (stable response to similar prompts, reliable knowledge gaps in underrepresented domains). This stability reflects the unchanging nature of training dataโAI capabilities remain stable unless the system is retrained with new data.

Analyze how incentives influence choices that may result in policies with a range of costs and benefits for different groups.
Lesson 2.5 Alignment: Understanding AI capabilities reveals economic incentives and policy implications: Companies are incentivized to train AI on popular topics (benefiting mainstream users) but may underinvest in niche domains (disadvantaging specialized users). Students see how technological design choices create winners and losers.

Dimension 3: Evaluating Sources and Using Evidence

D3.1.9-12
Gather relevant information from multiple sources representing a wide range of views while using the origin, authority, structure, context, and corroboration to guide the selection.
Lesson 2.5 Alignment: Students gather information from multiple "sources" (different prompt types, different knowledge domains, different trials), considering context (common vs. niche topics, structured vs. vague prompts) to understand what each reveals about AI. Corroboration comes from running multiple trials to confirm patterns.
D3.2.9-12
Evaluate the credibility of a source by examining how experts value the source.
Lesson 2.5 Alignment: Students evaluate AI response credibility by examining quality, consistency, and accuracy across different domains and prompt structures. They essentially become "experts" evaluating this source through systematic testing: Where does AI provide credible information? Where should it not be trusted?
D3.3.9-12
Identify evidence that draws information from multiple sources to support the validity of an argument or to detect inconsistencies in evidence in order to revise or strengthen claims.
Lesson 2.5 Alignment: Comparing AI responses across conditions helps students detect patterns and inconsistencies: "AI gave detailed responses for mainstream topics but struggled with niche ones." Multiple trials (sources) strengthen or weaken claims about AI capabilities, requiring students to weigh evidence systematically.
D3.4.9-12
Refine claims and counterclaims attending to precision, significance, and knowledge conveyed through the claim while pointing out the strengths and limitations of both.
Lesson 2.5 Alignment: When students make claims ("AI responds better to structured prompts"), they must refine based on evidence: adding precision ("structured prompts with specific word counts yield more comprehensive responses"), acknowledging limitations ("but this pattern wasn't consistent for creative tasks"), strengthening arguments through careful observation.

Dimension 4: Communicating Conclusions and Taking Informed Action

D4.1.9-12
Construct arguments using precise and knowledgeable claims, with evidence from multiple sources, while acknowledging counterclaims and evidentiary weaknesses.
Lesson 2.5 Alignment: Field journal documentation prepares students to construct arguments (in Lesson 2.7 discussions) about AI capabilities using precise claims supported by evidence from multiple experimental trials. Students learn to acknowledge weaknesses: "I only tested 3 prompts in this category, so this pattern might not be fully representative."
D4.2.9-12
Construct explanations using sound reasoning, correct sequence (linear or non-linear), examples, and details with significant and pertinent information and data, while acknowledging the strengths and weaknesses of the explanation given its purpose.
Lesson 2.5 Alignment: Documentation requires constructing explanations for observed patterns using sound reasoning ("AI knows mainstream topics better because training data contains more examples"), sequencing observations logically, providing specific examples from trials, and acknowledging limitations in scope or sample size.
D4.3.9-12
Present adaptations of arguments and explanations that feature evocative ideas and perspectives on issues and topics to reach a range of audiences and venues outside the classroom using print and oral technologies and digital technologies.
Lesson 2.5 Alignment: Students prepare documentation that will be shared in class discussions (Lesson 2.7), adapting their observations for peer audiences. The investigation connects to real-world venues: understanding AI capabilities is relevant to anyone using these technologies for school, work, or personal information needs.
D4.6.9-12
Use disciplinary and interdisciplinary lenses to understand the characteristics and causes of local, regional, and global problems; instances of such problems in multiple contexts; and challenges and opportunities faced by those trying to address these problems over time and place.
Lesson 2.5 Alignment: Understanding AI capabilities and limitations through hands-on investigation provides foundation for analyzing social problems through multiple lenses: technological (system design constraints), economic (unequal benefits across user groups), ethical (appropriate deployment contexts), civic (regulation needs). Students understand characteristics and causes: AI limitations stem from training data boundaries, creating challenges for equitable access and opportunities for improvement through better training.
D4.7.9-12
Assess options for individual and collective action to address local, regional, and global problems by engaging in self-reflection, strategy identification, and complex causal reasoning.
Lesson 2.5 Alignment: Understanding AI strengths and limitations enables informed action: students can make better choices about when to use AI (leveraging strengths), when to seek human expertise (addressing limitations), and how to advocate for better AI systems (pushing for more diverse training data, clearer capability disclosures). Self-reflection about their own observations prepares informed, strategic technology use.
